CHIEF COMPLAINT:
Sore throat and difficulty swallowing.

HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS:
A 19-year-old college student presents to the clinic with a 3-day history of worsening severe sore throat, odynophagia (difficulty swallowing), and subjective fevers. He notes feeling extremely fatigued and has swollen glands in his neck. He denies any cough or rhinorrhea. He mentions several students in his dorm have been sick recently with similar symptoms.

PAST MEDICAL HISTORY:
Unremarkable. Up to date on all immunizations.

MEDICATIONS:
None.

ALLERGIES:
Penicillin (Rash).

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION:
Vitals: BP 115/75, HR 92, RR 16, Temp 101.8 F (oral).
General: Appears mildly ill, uncomfortable appearing.
HEENT: Pharynx is significantly erythematous. Bilateral tonsils are enlarged (3+) with dense white exudate. Uvula is midline.
Neck: Prominent, tender anterior and posterior cervical lymphadenopathy. No palpable masses.
Cardiovascular/Respiratory: Heart regular rate and rhythm. Lungs are clear to auscultation bilaterally.

LABORATORY TESTS:
Rapid strep antigen test: Positive
Monospot (heterophile antibody) test: Negative

ASSESSMENT AND PLAN:
1. Acute streptococcal pharyngitis. The clinical presentation and positive rapid test confirm Group A Strep infection. 
2. Due to the patient's penicillin allergy (rash), we will treat with Azithromycin. Prescribed Z-Pak (Azithromycin 500mg day 1, then 250mg days 2-5).
3. Patient advised to rest, hydrate, and use ibuprofen for fever and pain control. Counselled to return if symptoms worsen or if they develop difficulty breathing or opening their mouth (to rule out a peritonsillar abscess).
4. Advised to stay out of classes until afebrile for at least 24 hours.
